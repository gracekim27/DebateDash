Are there disparities in high school debate across gender & schooling?

We investigated the debate performance of students in the major debate tournaments across the US.

We controlled for gender and whether students went to a private school or public school, and calculated students’ Elo scores. Elo scores are a measure of how well someone performs.

Ultimately, we found that male students from private schools perform the best in high school debates.

Elo

But to understand this difference in performance, we have to understand the Elo metric, and what it actually measures.

A player’s Elo increases when they win and decreases when they lose. In our model, a student starts with an Elo of 400.

But why do women perform worse?

Sexism in Debate

"My teammates and I have been called out for wearing pants instead of skirts. We've been called out for being too aggressive." - Hannah Ni

"Female debaters are twice as likely to be called out for aggression. And if this happens, and they're much more likely to lose." - Beyond Resolved Study

"If my friends and I don't feel comfortable speaking in a debate round because of discriminatory backlash, how can we feel comfortable speaking up when we're older and break barriers?"

"I went to an all-female high school, where I was really fortunate to have a lot of female role models. So my first experience with sexism, really, was in high school debate, getting comments from judges about what my voice sounded like, or what I was wearing, things male debaters weren’t really getting comments on." - Megan Munce

"I wore a red dress, and the judge took off points because it was "too much"" - Kristina Humphrey

"I remember my coach was like, ‘you will wear this lipstick, and you will wear this skirt’ because you have to fit this role if you want people to even consider what you have to say. It’s not that she was buying into it—she was telling me that if I even wanted them to hear what I had to say to give myself a chance to win, I had to be a part of the expectations for females." - Cara Borgsmiller

"In a debate where I got particularly passionate, I was talking and making my points, and I was probably being a little louder than normal because I really believed in [my] arguments, On my ballot, [the judge] said that I sounded like a b*tch." - Candace Wang

"Guys will talk over me in rounds and discredit me. It’s really easy to get disregarded or treated as if what I’m saying is less valuable than what my white, male partner is saying." - Brooke Gemechu

"“Typically, the male students will get more of a pat on the back. The female students, on the other hand, are typically slapped in the face with ‘you need to speak up more,’ or ‘what you said wasn’t as poised as your opponents.’”" - Kim Ong

Comments like these coming from competitors and judges not only tangibly hurt a woman's chances of winning a debate round, but also discourage an environment where woman feel like they can be treated as equals.

Closing The Case



Many of the same disparities that occur in real life, happen in debate.


Resource and funding inequalities result in better competitive performance by private school students.


Existing stereotypes about women discourage their participation in debate.


Addressing these disparities is crucial to creating a more inclusive and equitable community both in and out of debate.

Extra Information!

We are looking at Lincoln-Douglas debaters from Octofinal bid tournaments to the Tournament of Champions. We chose Lincoln Douglas debate because it is a 1v1 format of debate that allows us to analyze things like the gender of individual debaters. We also chose to use octofinal bid tournament data because these are the largest, most difficult national tournaments of the year and this classification ensures that the tournaments are all of similar difficulty when we are calculating Elos.

We manually calculate Elos using this tournament data. We use learning constant K=100 and the following round multipliers: (prelim, 1.0), (quads, 1.1), (trips, 1.2), (dubs, 1.4), (octos, 1.6), (quarters, 1.8), (semis, 2.0). We omit finals data to simplify data collection.
